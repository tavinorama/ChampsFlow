# Organic Posts — Pre-Launch Marketing Strategy

> Owned by vp-marketing. Written 2026-05-01. Word cap 2500. Hard rule: no claims about capabilities not in PRD; no fake testimonials; no urgency language without a real deadline.

---

## TL;DR

Organic Posts is a privacy-respecting AI tool that drafts social posts for SMBs, then waits for the owner to review and approve before anything goes live. It is in pre-launch with an MVP target of 2026-07-02 and zero public presence today. The marketing mission for the next 6 weeks is: (1) get a landing page live by 2026-05-17 to collect a waitlist, (2) publish 3 content pieces that establish credibility in the privacy-first SMB social space, and (3) seed a warm audience of 100 signups before launch. The primary differentiator we will lead with is zero data retention on AI calls plus a human-confirms-before-publish model — no competing tool advertises both together. Channel mix is LinkedIn organic (founder-optional), X, and an SEO blog seeded by keyword research. All copy must be transparent about pre-launch status and aligned with FTC §5 and EU AI Act transparency obligations.

## Positioning statement

Organic Posts is the social media scheduling tool for small businesses that want AI-drafted content they actually control — with zero-data-retention AI and a confirm-before-publish step built in.

## Target ICP

Three segments, ranked by conversion likelihood pre-launch:

**Segment A — The Time-Starved SMB Owner (primary)**
- Profile: 1–10 person business, owner manages their own social presence, posts inconsistently, finds the blank-page problem more painful than the scheduling problem. Verticals: local professional services (accountants, therapists, consultants), e-commerce DTC brands, trades businesses with a social presence.
- Pain: "I know I should post but I never know what to say and I'm scared it'll sound wrong."
- Why draft-and-confirm resonates: owner stays in control; the AI does the heavy lift but the owner has final say — this is not autopilot, which many SMB owners distrust.
- Why ZDR resonates: once you explain that most AI tools train on your data, they immediately care. "My client conversations stay mine."

**Segment B — The Solo Marketer at an SMB (secondary)**
- Profile: marketing generalist at a 10–50 person company, managing 2–4 brand accounts, probably already using Buffer or Later, frustrated by the copy workflow (tools schedule but don't draft).
- Pain: "I spend three hours a week writing captions. The scheduling is fine; the writing is the problem."
- Why ZDR resonates: responsible for brand reputation; GDPR-compliance claims from the tool reduce their internal audit burden.

**Segment C — The Freelance Social Media Manager (tertiary for pre-launch, valuable for word-of-mouth)**
- Profile: manages 3–8 client accounts, already sells "social media management" as a service, concerned about AI diluting their value but curious about efficiency gains.
- Why draft-and-confirm resonates: positions them as the confirming expert, not the one being replaced. They keep creative control.
- Why ZDR resonates: they are processing client brand data; GDPR processor obligations are real for EU-based freelancers.

**ICP for pre-launch targeting (default):** Segment A owner-managers who are EU or US-based, follow SMB or entrepreneurship communities on LinkedIn/X, and have expressed frustration with social media consistency.

## Channel mix

**LinkedIn Organic — Priority 1**
Rationale: Segment A and B are reachable on LinkedIn. B2B SaaS pre-launch demand is most efficiently built on LinkedIn through founder or brand posts that explain the problem, not just the product. Organic reach is still strong for accounts with no followers if the hook is specific. Content type: short-form posts (150–300 words), carousels (5–8 slides), and native polls to validate ICP pain points.

**X (Twitter) — Priority 2**
Rationale: Indie Hackers adjacent community, build-in-public narrative, SMB founder discourse. Useful for building-in-public posts during the pre-launch window (progress updates, waitlist milestones, design decisions). Lower conversion intent than LinkedIn but valuable for word-of-mouth amplification and community seeding. Content type: short threads, milestone posts, honest product progress updates.

**SEO Blog — Priority 3 (foundation-building, not immediate traffic)**
Rationale: Blog content will not rank within the pre-launch window. Publish now to build domain age and index history before launch. Keyword targets to be confirmed by seo-agent (dispatched this session). Blog pieces serve double duty as long-form social content repurposed to LinkedIn and X. Target: 1 article before landing page goes live (problem-statement piece to drive waitlist), 2 more in the 6-week pre-launch window.

**Channels NOT in scope pre-launch:** paid social (no budget approved, founder must approve campaigns before launch), email newsletter (no list yet — waitlist collection first), Product Hunt (hold for launch day).

## Brand voice guidelines

**Tone:** Straightforward and honest. We say what the product does and what it does not do. We write like a founder talking to a business owner, not a marketing department writing for a target segment.

**Person and tense:** Second person ("you"), present tense. Avoid future tense for features not yet shipped — use "coming soon" sparingly and only when there is a real roadmap commitment.

**Vocabulary do's:**
- Draft, review, approve, confirm, publish, control, consistent, honest, transparent, privacy, yours
- "We" for the company (warm, human). "You" for the reader.
- Plain English for any technical concept (explain ZDR as "the AI never sees your content after the call ends and never trains on it")

**Vocabulary don'ts:**
- "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "the only," "guaranteed," "best-in-class" — unsubstantiated superlatives
- "Never worry again," "effortless," "hands-off" — these undermine the draft-and-confirm positioning; the product requires human involvement by design
- "Your data is 100% safe" — overclaim; say "protected" with specific factual basis
- Any urgency language not tied to a real deadline ("limited spots," "closing soon," "offer expires") — violates honesty constraint and FTC §5
- Competitor names in copy unless the claim is factually verified and legally reviewed

**On AI:** Always describe AI as a drafting assistant, not an autonomous publisher. The human is in the loop — lead with that.

**On pre-launch status:** Be transparent. "We're building this now" and "join the waitlist to be first" are both honest and compelling. Do not imply the product is live if it is not.

## Honesty constraint reminder

This section is non-negotiable and applies to every content piece produced under this strategy.

1. No fake testimonials or fabricated social proof. If real users provide testimonials post-beta, they must be attributed with explicit consent.
2. Transparent about pre-launch state at all times. Never imply the product is available when it is not.
3. All AI capability claims must be grounded in what is in the PRD. Do not claim features on the roadmap as if they exist today.
4. Urgency or scarcity language is prohibited unless there is a real, verifiable deadline or capacity constraint. Waitlist "closing" or "limited spots" must be true if stated.
5. Competitor comparisons must be factually verifiable at time of publication. Run comparisons past VP Legal before publishing.
6. FTC §5 substantiation: every marketing claim must have a factual basis. Content-writer must include a "claim basis" note for each factual assertion in their draft; VP Marketing reviews before approving.
7. EU AI Act Article 13 transparency: any content that describes AI functionality must accurately represent what the system does, including limitations. The draft-and-confirm step must be presented as design intent, not marketing language.
